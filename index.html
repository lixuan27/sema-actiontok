    Action representation for robot policies

    An action tokenizer should align embodiments, not just compress actions.

    Train one shared action encoder on mixed-robot data. Let the short prefix of every code be a motion language every robot speaks — and let extra tokens appear only where precise execution truly demands them.

    Discretizing actions is now standard. But a better compressor is not a research contribution. The open question is whether the token space itself can become the place where different bodies — arms, bimanual rigs, dexterous hands, even humans — are put into correspondence, and whether a policy trained in that space keeps the fidelity of the best continuous controllers.

    Task

    One vocabulary in, every robot out.

    Given an expert action chunk from any robot — plus its cheap proprioceptive state and a structured descriptor of its body — learn a pair of maps.

    Tokenizer. Encode the chunk into an ordered, variable-length sequence of discrete tokens drawn from a single vocabulary shared by every embodiment, terminated by an end-of-sequence marker the model chooses for itself.

    Detokenizer. Turn any prefix of that sequence, together with the body descriptor, back into a finite, correctly-shaped, in-range command for that specific robot.

    The tokenizer is not judged on reconstruction. It is judged as the action interface of a policy: freeze it, train an autoregressive policy on its tokens, and measure closed-loop success in simulation.

    Four properties, held simultaneously

    SharedOne vocabulary across all bodies — otherwise the cross-embodiment claim is void.
    Semantic-firstThe first token carries reusable coarse intent, not residual detail.
    Variable-lengthLength tracks irreducible uncertainty, chosen per sample.
    Totally decodableEvery prefix — even a random one — decodes to a legal action.

    The three-way tension we optimize under

    Minimize distortion, subject to: few tokens per chunk (rate and inference latency), high closed-loop success (capability), and held-out bodies reaching ≈90% of a fully fine-tuned model (transfer). A method that wins one axis by sacrificing another has not solved the task.

    Framing

    The tokenizer is where bodies get aligned — or it is nothing.

    Four commitments separate an alignment mechanism from a codec. We take all four, and they set the entire experimental program.

    Joint, not bolted on

    The tokenizer enters policy pre-training and joint training — not a last-minute swap of the action head at fine-tuning time. The action space the policy learns in is the shared space.

    Mixed data, one encoder

    Robots with different action dimensions, control rates and kinematics are trained together on one encoder and one latent space; embodiment-aware decoders recover each robot's native commands.

    Honest about latency

    Discrete training is fast; autoregressive decoding is not free. Every result carries tokens-per-chunk and wall-clock decode cost beside success rate.

    Beat continuous control

    We compare against strong continuous policies (diffusion / flow-matching), not only against other tokenizers. A discrete interface has to earn its place.

    Where coarse-to-fine has to prove itself

    Ordering is cheap to claim on a 7-DoF arm, where two tokens already reconstruct most of the motion. Its real test is dexterous manipulation: a 20+ DoF hand where coarse intent ("close around the handle") and fine execution (per-finger contact timing) genuinely live at different scales. That is where a progressive code should pay, and where we commit to showing it.

    The data split has to mean something

    Random splits let a tokenizer memorize a body. Ours are semantic: held-out embodiments, held-out skills, and held-out control rates. The headline protocol — leave-one-embodiment-out — freezes the shared vocabulary and permits only a ≤5% adapter on the unseen robot.

    Diagnostic finding · our reproduction arm

    We built the variational variable-length coder first. Its adaptivity is a mirage.

    Before designing anything, we implemented and trained the strongest variable-length variational tokenizer we could, at full scale (100M samples, three seeds), on 669k action chunks.

    It reconstructs well. It does not do what its length mechanism promises: the encoder almost never chooses to stop early, its prefixes are not ordered, and most of its full-length decodes fall outside the legal action range.

    This is the finding that defines our design. An end-of-sequence token alone does not buy adaptive computation. Length must be structurally forced to mean something, and decodability must be guaranteed by construction — not hoped for.

    Insight

    Order carries transfer. Length carries uncertainty. Semantics come free at deployment.

    Insight A

    Ordering is the right carrier of cross-body transfer.

    Force tokens to run coarse-to-fine and the short prefix becomes body-independent motion semantics — grasp, lift, move left. Body-specific execution residual is pushed into the tail. One shared vocabulary plus a light per-robot adapter then suffices: sample complexity drops from per-body to shared.

    Sparked by: nested-dropout ordering, which is decisive for downstream control (removing it costs ~37% success); Matryoshka representations; progressive coding, where code length tracks −log p.

    Insight B

    A token is worth spending only if it reduces irreducible uncertainty.

    Length must be explained by how hard the motion is, not by which robot produced it. If body identity predicts length, the shared-vocabulary claim is contaminated. So we force a real stop decision — and then we try to falsify it with a pre-registered attribution test.

    Sparked by: our own negative result — a learned EOS that saturates at maximum length is a fixed-depth encoder wearing a costume.

    Insight C

    Semantics can be distilled from actions alone.

    Language and future-consequence supervision shape the first token during pre-training only. At deployment, tokenizing an expert action runs no vision-language model and no image encoder — it reads actions and state. Semantic structure, zero inference cost.

    Sparked by: semantic supervision of coarse streams improving grounding and long-horizon success; future-feature prediction accelerating convergence.

    Each insight is stated so it can be killed: A fails if ordering does not transfer; B fails if length regresses on body identity; C fails if semantic supervision costs more reconstruction than it returns downstream.

    Novelty

    One contribution, on one axis.

    A semantic-first progressive code whose short prefix is a cross-embodiment motion language, and whose residual tokens appear only where precise execution is irreducibly needed.

    It is a single claim with three limbs that support each other, not a bag of tricks:

    archShared vocabulary and a two-level decoder: one canonical motion decoder for every body, a thin adapter per body.
    methodA mandatory semantic first token, a structurally forced stop, and a bounded projection that makes every prefix decodable by construction.
    strategyFreeze the vocabulary on an unseen robot and train only a ≤5% adapter — transfer is an evaluation protocol, not an anecdote.

    Three evaluations this field does not currently run

    Leave-one-embodiment-out Freeze the shared vocabulary and canonical decoder; train only the ≤5% adapter on a body never seen. Claim embodiment-agnostic only above 90% of full fine-tuning — otherwise we downgrade the word to multi-embodiment, in writing.
    Length attribution Regress code length on reducible uncertainty versus body ID versus action magnitude. Pre-registered: if body ID dominates, our own central hypothesis is reported as falsified.
    Total decodability rate Fraction of arbitrary token sequences — including random ones — that decode in-range. Target 100%. No prior tokenizer reports this; the one we measured scores 28.8%.

    Related work is positioned, not padded: ordered tokenization (OAT) established that coarse-to-fine order matters for control. We inherit that lesson, and add the two things it does not have — a learned stop, and a vocabulary that outlives the robot it was trained on.

    Architecture

    Analytic where physics allows. Learned where it does not.

    select a stage

    Two levels, one vocabulary

    Every body is lifted into one canonical motion space by a map that is mostly closed-form — frames, units and control rates are physics, not something a network should have to learn. Only the true dynamic residual is left to a thin per-body adapter.

    The shared encoder then produces a single ordered code. On an unseen robot we freeze the vocabulary and the shared decoder, and train the adapter alone.

    Mechanisms

    What forces each property to actually hold.

    semantic-first

    The first token cannot be a stop

    Position one is barred from ending the sequence, so every code opens with coarse intent. Two lightweight heads — one aligning that token to a frozen language embedding of the task, one predicting the visual consequence of the motion — shape it during pre-training, then are thrown away.

    cross-embodiment

    Canonical space + body descriptor

    A fixed-length descriptor states the body's DoF, control mode, end-effector type, topology and rate. Randomly dropping it during training forces the shared decoder to fall back on a generic body — which is exactly the zero-shot condition on a robot it has never seen.

    ordered & safe

    Nested dropout, and a stop that must arrive

    Training randomly truncates the code and demands full reconstruction anyway, so prefixes are forced coarse-to-fine. The stop is guaranteed by the horizon. A final projection — mask, clamp, rotation re-orthogonalization — makes an illegal action unrepresentable rather than unlikely.

    optimization

    Keep the trunk; audit the alternative

    Variable-length latents are non-differentiable, so the trunk stays a sampled rollout with a clipped policy objective. A low-variance differentiable relaxation runs as a controlled ablation. Whichever wins on stability at equal quality is documented — the headline model is not silently swapped.

    Monitors that can abort a run

    Length distribution and stop-position histogram; fraction never stopping; per-step divergence; codebook utilization and entropy; reconstruction. Any of minimum-length collapse, length saturation, divergence blow-up, or utilization below 20% halts the run rather than producing a number we would have to explain away later.

    What we refuse to do

    Monotonic prefix improvement is treated as a property to verify, never enforced as a hard rule. Success is reported against pre-registered criteria fixed before the runs. If length turns out to be explained by body identity, that is written up as a refutation of our own hypothesis.

    Training

    Three stages, and the tokenizer is inside the policy — not beside it.

    stage 1

    Tokenizer pre-training

    Action-only, from scratch, tiny model. Warm-start on two bodies, then joint training on all bodies with temperature-balanced sampling and body dropout. Semantic heads active here and only here.

    Cost: 1–2 GPUs per arm, many arms in parallel. Small models do not get eight cards.

    stage 2

    Policy training in the shared space

    Freeze the tokenizer; the policy is trained autoregressively over its tokens from the start, with the stop token driving early termination. Targets are sampled fresh from the posterior every batch — never a stale argmax.

    Matched by construction: every tokenizer we compare gets the same vision encoder, the same transformer, the same data, the same 50k steps, the same parameter budget.

    stage 3

    Transfer and the dexterous test

    Freeze the vocabulary and shared decoder; adapt an unseen body with ≤5% of parameters. Then take the coarse-to-fine claim to a high-DoF hand, where a progressive code has something real to buy.

    Zero-shot, adapter-only, from-scratch and full fine-tuning are all reported side by side.

    Evaluation — three layers, one verdict

    IntrinsicRate–distortion across prefixes, code length distribution, codebook use, decode latency, total decodability, noise robustness.
    Controlled downstream verdictFrozen tokenizer, matched policy, closed-loop success in simulation. Three seeds, mean ± standard error. This is the only table that decides anything.
    Cross-bodyLeave-one-embodiment-out, shared-vocabulary retention, cross-body token retrieval, robustness to control rate and dimension.

    Who we run against

    Per-dimension binning · frequency+BPE · fixed-depth VQ and residual VQ · ordered tokenization with an externally chosen length · a shared-trunk cross-body codec · and strong continuous controllers (diffusion / flow-matching policies), because a discrete interface that loses to continuous control has not made its case.

    Latency is a first-class column: success rate is always reported beside tokens-per-chunk and wall-clock decode time. Winning on success while tripling inference cost is not winning.

    Plan

    Six phases. Every one has a gate that can stop it.

    A gate is a number agreed before the run. If it is missed, the next phase does not start — we diagnose, or we change the hypothesis. Open a phase for its checkpoints and its contingency.

    Increments

    One mechanism at a time. Each must earn its merge.

    Merge policy

    A round enters the trunk only if it moves controlled downstream success by more than 1%. Otherwise it is rolled back and the reason is recorded. No round is merged on reconstruction alone.

    RoundWhat is addedMerge test
    R0Faithful variable-length baseline, no changesReproduce published success band
    R1Ordered prefixes: nested dropout, causal latents, prefix lossesPrefix improvement becomes the norm, not the exception
    R2Semantic-first token + pre-training-only supervisionEarly-token skill probe up; body probe down
    R3Cross-embodiment: canonical space, shared vocab, adaptersHeld-out body reaches 90% of full fine-tuning
    R4Low-variance optimizer (conditional)Equal quality at lower variance, or it stays an appendix

    Pre-registered success ladder

    L1Coherent. No divergence; every legal token sequence decodes finite and in range; monitors healthy. reached
    L2Effective. Baseline reproduced within 5%; first model reaches 90% of it. reached
    L3Competitive. Within 5% of the strongest matched baseline on both benchmarks — with cross-body transfer as pure net gain.
    L4State of the art. Beats the strongest matched baseline on ≥2 benchmarks over 3 seeds — or matches it with 20% fewer tokens and lower latency.
    L5Beyond. Ablations isolate each mechanism; held-out body ≥90%; length is shown to follow uncertainty, not body identity.

    The ladder was fixed in the design freeze and is not editable after the fact. L1 and L2 are closed on real runs; L3 upward is the work ahead.

    Risk register

    Five ways this fails, and what we do on the day it does.

    Sampled optimization is unstable

    high

    Plan: keep the published stabilizers exactly; run the collapse monitors with hard abort thresholds; have the differentiable halting path implemented before it is needed, not after.

    Semantics cost more reconstruction than they return

    medium

    Plan: position-aware weighting confines semantic pressure to early tokens; an action-only arm runs in every ablation; the verdict is downstream success, never reconstruction.

    The shared vocabulary collapses to an average body

    medium

    Plan: align bodies analytically before any learning; drop the descriptor during training; ablate descriptor against body ID to locate the cause. If held-out transfer misses 90%, we say multi-embodiment and stop saying agnostic.

    Length is explained by the robot, not the motion

    medium

    Plan: the attribution regression is pre-registered. If body ID dominates, the central hypothesis is reported as falsified and the contribution narrows to a length-as-complexity-prior result. Written before the number exists.

    Evaluation throughput eats the schedule

    medium

    Plan: rollouts are rendering-bound at minutes per episode. Full matrix cells run at 20 rollouts, headline cells at 50; evaluation is parallelized across many single-GPU jobs rather than made faster per job. Budget is computed before submission, not discovered at timeout.

    Second-body infrastructure drifts

    low

    Plan: the tokenizer for a new body needs actions only — no simulator. The simulator is needed only for its policy rollouts, so it is built as an isolated environment and can never break the harness that already works.
